The King of the Fourth

An Interactive Feature on Isaiah Thomas

By Adam MacDonald

Isaiah Thomas has been a revelation this season. Despite standing at just 5'9'', Thomas has carried the Boston Celtics nightly scoring burden — single-handedly helping an oft-injured, star-deficient team steal the top seed in the Eastern Conference.

Oh, and he also happened to toss up one of the greatest advanced-metrics seasons of all-time. Not bad for someone who's the same height as my mom.

The Numbers Don't Lie

There's a reason Thomas has been anointed King of the Fourth by his beantown brethren. He's 2nd in the league in fourth-quarter points per game, only trailing Russell Westbrook, but doing scoring with superior efficiency.

In fact, Thomas is having one of the most efficient high-volume scoring seasons ever. Out of players who've averaged at least 19 shots per game in a season, Thomas ranks 7th in true shooting percentage all-time and 2nd all-time among guards – only trailing Stephen Curryâ€™s 2015-16 MVP season.

How is he doing this?

A lot of Thomas' offensive production comes off of pick-and-roll, isolations, and ferocious transition sprints, but he's also evolved to be an off-ball threat.

Brad Stevens, head coach of the Boston Celtics, installed sets running Thomas through screens to get him into a "live dribble," a situation where the player receives the ball and immediately goes into action, such as a shot or dribble penetration.

This video is a common variant of this set where Thomas starts off in the right corner and runs through a screen. As that is happening, a player at the top of the key dribbles towards Thomas for the handoff.

The defender then gets stuck in a conundrum. If the defender goes under the screen, Thomas can pull up from 3. If he goes over, Thomas can attack the rim to finish or draw fouls. If the defense denies lane entry, Thomas can kick it to an open man.
